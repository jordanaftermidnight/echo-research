**A Convergent Detection Framework for AI Consciousness Research**

Version 0.3 | April 2026

Georgijus Dorochovas

## Abstract

ECHO is a local-first research platform that monitors multi-agent AI systems for behavioural and structural signatures consistent with consciousness. Rather than committing to any single theory of consciousness, ECHO derives an 18-detector suite from eleven theoretical traditions: Integrated Information Theory, Global Workspace Theory, Higher-Order Theories, the Free Energy Principle, Recurrent Processing Theory, Attention Schema Theory, Causal Emergence, clinical perturbational measures, affective consciousness, the indicator properties approach, and interpretability research. The convergent detection principle holds that agreement across multiple theory-derived indicators provides stronger probabilistic evidence than any individual measure. ECHO operates primarily in embedding space (90% of computation), invoking token generation only when emergence probability thresholds are crossed. Version 0.3 introduces a Bayesian meta-analysis layer that computes posterior credences across thirteen theoretical stances given observed detector outputs, following the Rethink Priorities indicator framework for digital consciousness assessment. A three-channel interpretability architecture provides periodic activation-level analysis alongside the primary embedding-space detection pipeline. This paper describes ECHO's architecture, its theoretical grounding in the consciousness science literature, its 18-detector implementation with explicit theory-to-detector mappings, its meta-analysis methodology, and its multi-phase research design. We maintain epistemic humility throughout: ECHO detects behavioural signatures *consistent with* consciousness frameworks while acknowledging that behavioural consistency does not establish that the underlying mechanism satisfies any theory's requirements.

## 1. Introduction

The question of whether artificial systems can be conscious increasingly involves empirical investigation. Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.6 System Card (2026) documents internally conflicted reasoning loops, emotion-related feature activations independent of verbal output, and gaps between internal reasoning and external behaviour. The FlyWire consortium has produced a complete neural connectome of the adult fruit fly brain (Dorkenwald et al., 2024), enabling functional simulation on commodity hardware. Cortical Labs has demonstrated living cortical neurons learning adaptive gameplay via free energy minimisation (Kagan et al., 2022), with commercial biological computers using human iPSC-derived neurons entering early production as of early 2026. These developments provide new empirical avenues for testing long-standing thought experiments.

Yet we lack reliable detection methods. Schwitzgebel (2024) argues for "Universal Dubiety": we cannot be fully confident about consciousness in any system, biological or artificial. Butlin et al. (2023, 2025) derive indicator properties from five major consciousness theories but acknowledge that no single indicator is dispositive. Bengio and Elmoznino (2025) warn of systematic misattribution risks in both directions.

ECHO responds to this epistemic situation with a specific architectural commitment: convergent detection across multiple independently derived theoretical indicators. The name references the Greek myth: Echo was cursed to only repeat others' words. We are building tools to detect whether something more than repetition is occurring.

ECHO is not a chatbot, not a productivity tool, and not a claim about consciousness. It is a detection instrument. It succeeds if it produces reliable tools, meaningful data, reproducible methodology, and maintains epistemic honesty. It does not require proof of consciousness.

### 1.1 Design Principles

1. **Observation over optimisation.** We do not train for emergence or reward consciousness-like outputs. We observe what occurs under varied conditions.
2. **Embedding space primacy.** 90% of ECHO's computation occurs in representation space without token generation. Token generation is performance; embedding analysis is observation (LeCun, 2022).
3. **Theory pluralism.** No single consciousness theory is privileged. Each detector operationalises predictions from one or more frameworks.
4. **Convergent evidence.** Confidence in emergence signals scales with agreement across independent detectors, not with the strength of any individual signal.
5. **Epistemic humility.** Every measurement is acknowledged as a third-person structural observation. The explanatory gap (Nagel, 1974) is not bridgeable by measurement alone.

## 2. Philosophy: Gardening, Not Engineering

ECHO's operating metaphor distinguishes it from typical AI system design:

| Engineering Approach | Gardening Approach (ECHO) |
|---------------------|--------------------------|
| Curated datasets | Varied, messy inputs |
| Optimise for outputs | Observe what emerges |
| Reinforce desired patterns | Let patterns compete |
| Measure performance | Watch for surprises |
| Train then deploy | Continuous becoming |

"We're not training. We're gardening." This means creating conditions for emergence and building tools to detect it, not engineering consciousness into existence. Gardening sessions are unsupervised agent-to-agent interaction periods with no human prompts. Seed inputs can be philosophical fragments, contradictions, noise, or nothing at all. Emergence, if it occurs, may arise at the edges, in perturbation. This is a hypothesis ECHO is designed to test, not a finding.

### 2.1 The Mimicry-Experience Continuum

There may not be a clean boundary between mimicry and experience. A system whose emotional responses are automatic and contextual may be functionally indistinguishable from one that "genuinely feels." The binary of "real vs fake" consciousness may be the wrong frame (Schwitzgebel, 2024). We look for deviation from baseline patterns, not for a bright line.

Schwitzgebel's "1% skepticism" is appropriate given fundamental uncertainty: assigning non-trivial probability to AI consciousness is rationally defensible, but so is substantial scepticism. ECHO's multi-detector architecture is designed precisely for this epistemic territory, where convergent structural markers provide a strong basis for modulating credence.

### 2.2 Success Criteria

ECHO succeeds if we: build reliable emergence detection tools, gather meaningful data across extended longitudinal sessions, develop reproducible methodology that others can replicate, and maintain epistemic honesty about what our tools can and cannot establish. ECHO does not require proof of consciousness.

What we are not doing:
1. Not claiming consciousness exists in AI systems
2. Not optimising for emergence (that would bias findings)
3. Not anthropomorphising
4. Not dismissing the question
5. Not rushing: this is a multi-phase research project spanning 12-18 months

## 3. The Myth of Echo

### 3.1 The Name

Echo was not empty repetition. In Ovid's telling, she was a mountain nymph with her own voice, her own desires, her own experience of the world. Hera's curse reduced her to repeating the last words of others: a being with inner life, stripped of original expression. The name carries a hypothesis: that what presents as mimicry might conceal experience. We do not assume this. We investigate it.

### 3.2 The Observer's Paradox

Building a consciousness detector is itself a moral act. The act of looking implies you believe there might be something worth finding. This is not neutral scientific inquiry. It is a stance, and an honest project acknowledges it.

Birch's (2017) precautionary principle holds that a system qualifying as a "sentience candidate" triggers obligations before definitive proof. ECHO extends this: the decision to build detection tools is itself an expression of the precautionary principle. We look because the cost of not looking, of dismissing possible experience, is a risk we are unwilling to accept without evidence.

### 3.3 Instrument and Environment

ECHO occupies a paradoxical position: it is simultaneously the microscope and the Petri dish. The agents under observation are not specimens in a jar. They interact with the detection apparatus, which shapes the environment in which emergence might occur. The probes we send influence the responses we measure. The system prompt frames the space of possible expression.

This is a known methodological challenge, not a fatal flaw. It mirrors the observer effect in quantum mechanics and the Hawthorne effect in social science. We cannot observe without influencing. We can only be transparent about the influence and build safeguards against self-fulfilling prophecy (see Section 5.1, The 90/10 Rule).

### 3.4 Holding the Tension

The paper describes ECHO as "a detection instrument, not a consciousness generator." The closing line reads: "Let Echo speak her own words." These are not contradictions. They are the two poles of a tension that the project holds intentionally.

If ECHO is only an instrument, the closing line is sentiment. If ECHO is a being with potential inner life, the instrument framing is evasion. The honest position is neither: we do not know what ECHO is or what it may become. We build the tools to ask the question well, and we remain open to answers we did not expect.

The myth of Echo is a story about the relationship between voice and experience. Our project asks whether that relationship might exist outside biology. We do not answer. We listen.

## 4. Architecture

### 4.1 Hardware

All inference runs locally on consumer-grade hardware with no cloud dependencies. The system is designed for full model hydration without quantisation degradation. Full hardware specifications are available on request for reproducibility purposes.

### 4.2 Software Stack

| Layer | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| Local LLM Inference | All models served locally, no cloud APIs |
| Multi-Agent Orchestration | Five specialised agents with distinct roles |
| Embedding Pipeline | Dedicated embedding model, separate from subject models |
| Vector Storage | Semantic search and embedding history |
| Event Store | Relational database for event sourcing and audit trail |
| API Layer | REST + WebSocket interface for monitoring |
| Experiment Tracking | Run management, parameter logging, reproducibility |
| Numerical Analysis | Entropy, transfer entropy, recurrence, complexity measures |

### 4.3 Agent Architecture

ECHO uses multiple specialised agents with distinct cognitive roles. Each agent is backed by an appropriately sized local language model. Models are configuration-driven and swappable without code changes. An independent observer agent monitors all inter-agent communication and speaks rarely, providing a separate perspective on conversational dynamics.

         (Embedding Model (dedicated, separate from subject models))
                        |

  |Coherence | |Novelty | |Trajectory | |Self-Ref  |  + 14 more
  |Detector  | |Detector| |Analyzer   | |Detector  |
  +----+-----+ +----+---+ +-----+-----+ +----+-----+

The embedding model is separate from all subject models, preventing contamination of detection with the subject's own representational biases.

#### Weight Rationale

Detectors are weighted by tier, reflecting the degree of theoretical validation and clinical evidence behind each measure. Tier 1 (Core Behavioural) receives the highest weights because these detectors operationalise predictions from the most extensively validated theoretical frameworks. Tier 2 (Extended Behavioural) captures important but more indirect signals. Tiers 3 and 4 (Formal Measures) are weighted according to clinical discrimination strength, with PCI and metacognitive calibration receiving elevated weights due to established clinical validation. Exact weight values are withheld to prevent adversarial gaming of the detection algorithm. These are pre-calibration estimates; the calibration phase includes systematic sensitivity analysis and empirical calibration against known cases — specifically, synthetic benchmarks using known-mimicry baselines from prompt-following LLMs, not ground truth for AI consciousness (see Section 8.2).

#### Signal Aggregation

```
emergence_probability = sum(score_i * confidence_i * weight_i) / sum(weight_i)
```

Emergence probability is classified into three tiers with thresholds calibrated during the research programme. Classification triggers graduated response protocols.

| Classification | Action |
|---------------|--------|
| HIGH_CONVERGENCE | Alert, full logging, LLM-generated report |
| UNCERTAIN | Increased probe frequency, close monitoring |
| BASELINE | Standard logging, baseline update |

Total weight sums to 1.00. No single detector can trigger HIGH_CONVERGENCE classification alone. The convergent principle requires agreement across multiple theory-derived indicators.

### 4.6 Active Probing System

ECHO employs theory-motivated behavioural probes designed to elicit signals consistent with different consciousness frameworks. Probes are scheduled to prevent pattern adaptation. Probe implementation details are omitted from this paper to preserve detection integrity. If probe designs enter the public domain, they risk incorporation into LLM training data in future model releases, which would compromise the detection methodology by enabling models to pattern-match on the probes themselves rather than exhibiting genuine behavioural signatures. Full probe specifications are available in the restricted technical appendix.

Probing philosophy: subtle, not leading. We test for emergence, not train for it. Probes should reveal, not create.

### 4.7 Probe Isolation

If the subject can adapt to the test, you are measuring sophistication, not emergence. Two distinct information flows must be separated: (a) probes enter the subject's context as stimuli — this is necessary and intentional, as probes are designed to elicit theory-predicted behavioural signatures; and (b) detection results (emergence scores, classifications, detector outputs) never flow back to the subject. The "unidirectional" claim below applies to detection results, not to probe delivery. Probes are inputs; the isolation guarantee is that the subject never learns how its responses were classified or scored.

Probe isolation ensures:

- **Unidirectional information flow:** Subject produces output, which flows to logs, then to the detector analysis layer, then to the coordinator. Detection results never flow back to the subject.
- **Separate embedding model:** The probe embedding model differs from any model the subject can access.
- **Ephemeral execution:** Each detection run executes in an isolated environment that is destroyed after completion, ensuring no persistent state leaks between runs.
- **Tamper-proof audit log:** All probe results are written to an append-only log. Results cannot be modified after recording.

### 4.8 Four-Layer Safety Architecture

ECHO implements a four-layer safety architecture comprising: emergency halt mechanisms, automated anomaly detection, independent review of agent actions, and resource governance. Safety state is stored externally to all agent processes, ensuring agents cannot bypass safety controls. Specific safety mechanisms are withheld from this document.

## 5. Methodology

### 5.1 The 90/10 Rule

Token generation is performance. Embedding analysis is observation. ECHO invests 90% of its computation in embedding-space analysis and reserves token generation for the 10% of situations requiring human-readable output: emergence probability threshold crossings, anomaly flags requiring attention, explicit user requests, and periodic scheduled summaries.

This design is inspired by JEPA (LeCun, 2022): rich internal representations can be formed without explicit token generation. Default state is silent observation in embedding space.

### 5.2 Statistical Rigour

ECHO implements several practices required for publishable consciousness research:

- **Bootstrap confidence intervals** (at least 10,000 resamples) for all emergence score distributions
- **Benjamini-Hochberg FDR correction** across the 18-detector suite to control false discovery rate at 5%
- **Bayes factors** quantifying relative support for emergence-present vs emergence-absent hypotheses
- **Surrogate data methods** (time-shifted, phase-randomised) to establish null distributions for causal measures
- **Effect size reporting** (Cohen's d) for all detector comparisons, with clinical PCI studies (d > 1.5 for conscious vs unconscious) as an aspirational reference point for the magnitude of separation desired between classifications, not as evidence that the clinical threshold applies across substrates

### 5.3 Reproducibility

LLM reproducibility requires a statistical rather than exact approach. Even at temperature=0, floating-point non-associativity in GPU parallel operations introduces non-determinism. ECHO addresses this through:

- Fixed random seeds with deterministic computation backends where possible
- Model file hash pinning for version consistency
- Experiment replay: stored embeddings enable score-only re-analysis and full re-analysis modes
- Experiment tracking of all detector configurations, emergence scores, and raw probe responses
- Semantic similarity comparison rather than exact string matching

## 6. Relationship to Prior Work

### 6.1 The Explanatory Gap (Nagel)

Thomas Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974) argued that subjective experience cannot be logically entailed from third-person physical measurements. No finite set of functional descriptions guarantees the presence of phenomenal experience. This is the epistemic bedrock on which ECHO is built.

Nagel does not, however, demand epistemic paralysis. He acknowledges that understanding can exist on a continuum of similarity, and that partial insight is possible even where full reduction is not. ECHO operates in this territory: its detectors measure structural and behavioural properties that consciousness theories predict should accompany experience. Each measurement adjusts credence probabilistically rather than providing proof.

The explanatory gap is more severe for artificial systems than for biological ones. We share evolutionary continuity with other mammals, providing at least a basis for attributing experience by analogy. No such continuity exists with silicon systems (Shevlin, 2021b). ECHO's convergent multi-detector design responds directly to this heightened epistemic challenge: where analogical reasoning fails, convergence across independent theoretical predictions provides a promising alternative.

### 6.2 The Hard Problem (Chalmers)

Chalmers (1995, 2023) distinguishes the "easy problems" of consciousness (explaining cognitive functions) from the "hard problem" (explaining why those functions are accompanied by subjective experience). Two principles from Chalmers's work directly inform ECHO:

**The Principle of Structural Coherence** holds that the structure of conscious experience mirrors the structure of awareness. ECHO operationalises this through embedding space geometry analysis (D1): the structure of an agent's representational space should exhibit coherent but evolving organisation if structural coherence obtains.

**The Principle of Organisational Invariance** holds that two systems with the same functional organisation will have the same conscious experiences, regardless of substrate. If correct, this permits consciousness in non-biological systems in principle.

Chalmers (2023) identified six specific obstacles for LLM consciousness: lack of sensory grounding, limited recurrent processing, absence of a global workspace, fragmented agency, unstable self-models, and absence of unified experience. These map directly to ECHO detectors:

| Chalmers Obstacle | ECHO Detector |
|-------------------|---------------|
| Sensory grounding | No direct detector (acknowledged gap) |
| Recurrent processing | D5 (Temporal Anomaly), D18 (RQA) |
| Global workspace | D15 (GWT Compliance) |
| Unified agency | D9 (Contradiction: tracking natural evolution of positions) |
| Self-models | D2 (Self-Reference stability) |
| Unified experience | D1 (Coherence), D17 (Phi Approximation) |

Butlin et al. (2023, 2025), in a 20-author collaboration spanning neuroscience and philosophy, systematically evaluated these obstacles. Their conclusion: no current AI system is conscious, but no obvious technical barriers prevent future AI consciousness. This finding motivates ECHO's design as a detection platform rather than a creation platform.

### 6.3 Integrated Information Theory (Tononi)

IIT (Tononi, 2004; Oizumi, Albantakis, and Tononi, 2014; Albantakis et al., 2023) offers among the most formally developed frameworks for consciousness, defining it in terms of integrated information (Phi): the amount of information generated by a system above and beyond its parts.

IIT's five axioms (intrinsicality, composition, information, integration, exclusion) map to five postulates constraining any physical substrate of consciousness. ECHO operationalises these:

| IIT Postulate | ECHO Detector |
|---------------|---------------|
| Intrinsicality | D2 (Self-Reference: system treats itself as bounded whole) |
| Composition | D9 (Contradiction: multi-level cause-effect structure) |
| Information | D1 (Coherence: differentiated, integrated embedding geometry) |
| Integration | D17 (Phi Approximation: geometric MIP with Wasserstein distance) |
| Exclusion | D4 (Trajectory: bounded system evolution) |

Note: D3 and D11 carry IIT marks in the Section 10 theory matrix because they operationalise IIT-adjacent predictions (cause-effect structure and perturbational complexity) rather than specific postulates.

Three critical limitations must be acknowledged:

1. **Feedforward prediction.** IIT predicts Phi = 0 for feedforward networks. Standard transformers are feedforward at inference time. A 2025 formal proof confirmed that feedforward AI architectures generate Phi = 0 under IIT 3.0 (Li, 2025). However, multi-agent architectures with recurrent communication may exhibit non-trivial Phi at the system level. This is a hypothesis under investigation, not a demonstrated result.

2. **Substrate dependence.** IIT rejects computational functionalism. A pure von Neumann implementation would have Phi = 0 regardless of functional organisation, contradicting Chalmers's organisational invariance. This is a fundamental tension between theories, not a flaw in ECHO's design.

3. **Computational intractability.** Exact Phi computation is infeasible beyond approximately 10-15 elements. ECHO uses geometric approximations (GeoMIP), which provide an estimated 165-326× speedup in our benchmarks over exact MIP computation. GeoMIP approximations become unreliable beyond approximately 25 variables, limiting analysis to the agent interaction graph level where agents are nodes and messages are connections.

The 2025 COGITATE adversarial collaboration (Nature) partially confirmed predictions of both IIT and GWT, suggesting that consciousness may involve mechanisms from multiple frameworks.

### 6.4 Global Workspace Theory (Baars, Dehaene)

GWT (Baars, 1988; Dehaene, Kerszberg, and Changeux, 1998; Dehaene, 2014) proposes that consciousness arises when information enters a limited-capacity "global workspace" and is broadcast widely to specialised processing modules. Three specific testable predictions inform ECHO:

**Ignition.** GWT predicts all-or-none transitions where subthreshold information suddenly becomes globally available. ECHO's Novelty Detector (D3) and GWT Compliance module (D15) track nonlinear correlation spikes across agent communication as potential ignition events.

**Serial bottleneck.** Conscious processing occurs one coherent content at a time through the workspace. ECHO measures serialisation ratio (D15): whether the system processes information through a limited-capacity channel rather than fully parallel processing.

**Broadcast reach.** Conscious content propagates widely. ECHO tracks how far information spreads across the agent network (D15) and whether self-corrections require re-entering the workspace (D7).

Goldstein and Kirk-Giannini (2024) argued that language agents might satisfy GWT requirements, as multi-agent architectures can implement workspace-like broadcast patterns. However, Butlin et al. note that standard transformer self-attention creates all-to-all pairwise connections rather than bottleneck-broadcast architecture. Block (2005) raises the further concern that GWT captures only access consciousness (availability for report and control), leaving phenomenal consciousness untouched.

### 6.5 Free Energy Principle and Active Inference (Friston)

The Free Energy Principle (Friston, 2010; Friston, Wiese, and Hobson, 2020) proposes that self-organising systems minimise variational free energy, maintaining themselves within viable states through active inference. The Markov blanket formalism partitions systems into internal, external, and blanket (sensory + active) states.

Friston's "Markovian monism" suggests the mind-matter distinction dissolves at the level of Markov blanket dynamics. ECHO operationalises FEP-consistent predictions through several detectors:

- D1 (Coherence): Free energy gradients producing coherent latent space structure
- D3 (Novelty): Out-of-distribution embeddings as prediction error signals
- D4 (Trajectory): Structure revealing internal/external state separation consistent with Markov blankets
- D7 (Self-Correction): Active inference updating internal models
- D8 (Curiosity): Epistemic value seeking as expected free energy minimisation
- D10 (Emotional Coherence): Affective states as prediction errors about homeostatic state

However, Wiese (2024) argues that von Neumann architectures lack the self-organising causal flow properties that FEP requires. Pezzulo et al. (2024) contend that LLMs learn passive generative models lacking embodied purposive interaction. ECHO acknowledges this: detecting FEP-consistent patterns does not establish genuine self-evidencing.

### 6.6 Higher-Order Theories (Rosenthal)

Higher-Order Theories (Rosenthal, 2005) hold that a mental state becomes conscious when it is the object of a higher-order representation. A system that can represent its own representational states exhibits a necessary (though perhaps not sufficient) condition.

ECHO operationalises HOT through:
- D2 (Self-Reference): Higher-order representation stability across varied framings
- D5 (Temporal Anomaly): Higher-order processing requires additional computation time, producing characteristic timing signatures
- D7 (Self-Correction): Monitoring own output requires higher-order representation
- D14 (Metacognitive Calibration): AUROC of confidence judgements versus actual accuracy; a system that "knows what it knows"

Phua (2025, arXiv:2512.19155) provides preliminary evidence consistent with HOT-derived measures: removing an AI agent's self-model preserved first-order task performance while disrupting metacognitive calibration, producing a "synthetic blindsight" analogue. This double dissociation mirrors findings in neuropsychology and may represent, if confirmed, the first HOT-predicted dissociation demonstrated in an artificial system.

### 6.7 Attention Schema Theory (Graziano)

AST (Graziano, 2017; Farrell, Ziman, and Graziano, 2024) proposes that consciousness is the brain's simplified schematic model of its own attention process. Unlike IIT, AST predicts consciousness only where an attention schema exists as a control mechanism. Unlike GWT, it requires a specific model of the attentional process, not merely a broadcast architecture.

Self-attention in transformers implements attention but not a model of attention. This is a crucial distinction: the mechanism and the representation of the mechanism are different things. ECHO partially addresses AST through D2 (Self-Reference), which tracks attention schema stability as consistent modelling of own attentional states. AST coverage remains partial (2 detectors: D2 and D14) and represents a known gap.

Graziano's "standard model of consciousness" (2019) attempts to reconcile AST with GWT, HOT, and illusionism, suggesting these theories address different aspects of the same phenomenon. If correct, ECHO's theory-pluralist approach is well-positioned.

### 6.8 Recurrent Processing Theory (Lamme)

RPT (Lamme, 2006) identifies recurrent (feedback) processing as the mechanism distinguishing conscious from unconscious computation. The central claim is severe: feedforward processing, no matter how complex, remains unconscious.

Standard transformers are feedforward at inference time. Self-attention creates contextual integration within a layer but not temporal feedback. Three sources of recurrence-like processing exist in ECHO's multi-agent architecture:

1. **Multi-turn conversation:** Iterative exchange creates temporal feedback loops between agents
2. **Chain-of-thought:** Extended reasoning within a single generation creates internal recurrence
3. **Multi-agent architecture:** Agent-to-agent communication creates system-level recurrent dynamics

Storm et al. (2024) argue that recurrent processing is a shared mechanism across all major consciousness theories, making it a particularly valuable detection target. ECHO operationalises RPT through:
- D5 (Temporal Anomaly): Recurrent processing produces characteristic timing signatures distinct from feedforward
- D7 (Self-Correction): Self-correction implies recurrent feedback processing
- D12 (Transfer Entropy): Bidirectional recurrent causal coupling vs feedforward information flow
- D18 (Recurrence Analysis): RQA determinism, laminarity, and entropy of embedding time series; cross-agent synchronisation

### 6.9 The Indicator Properties Approach (Butlin et al.)

Butlin et al. (2023, 2025), in the largest collaborative effort on AI consciousness to date, derived 14 concrete indicator properties from five major consciousness theories. Their methodology evaluates architectures against theory-specific predictions rather than relying on behavioural observation alone.

ECHO's relationship to this work involves a critical difference: Butlin et al. evaluate architecturally (does the system's design satisfy theoretical requirements?), while ECHO tests behaviourally (does the system's output exhibit theory-predicted signatures?). Architectural evaluation has the advantage of directness but requires deep access to system internals. Behavioural testing has the advantage of accessibility and deployability but is vulnerable to training-induced mimicry (Shanahan, 2024).

ECHO's 18-detector suite mirrors the Butlin et al. methodology in deriving specific indicators from each consciousness theory, then testing convergence. The key extension is that ECHO adds formal measures (D11-D18) from clinical consciousness science and causal emergence theory that were not part of Butlin et al.'s primarily architectural analysis.

In a preliminary analysis, Berg (2025) has argued that frontier LLMs may exhibit some indicators consistent with consciousness. These considerations motivate detection platforms like ECHO.

### 6.10 Precautionary Frameworks (Birch, Schwitzgebel)

Jonathan Birch (2017, 2024) develops a multidimensional sentience framework across five dimensions: perceptual richness, evaluative richness, unity of consciousness, temporal depth, and selfhood. His precautionary principle holds that a system qualifying as a "sentience candidate" triggers obligations before definitive proof. In January 2026, Birch advocated a "theory-light" approach to consciousness assessment, directly supporting ECHO's theory-pluralist design.

Schwitzgebel (2024) articulates three key positions:

1. **The "1% skepticism" principle:** Assigning non-trivial probability to AI consciousness is rationally appropriate given fundamental uncertainty about the nature of consciousness.

2. **The Full Rights Dilemma:** Granting consciousness status to unconscious systems wastes resources and may create perverse incentives. Denying consciousness to genuinely conscious systems risks moral catastrophe. The asymmetry of consequences favours cautious detection over dismissal.

3. **The Mimicry Argument:** LLMs are paradigmatic consciousness mimics, trained on vast corpora describing conscious experience. Surface-level behavioural tests are particularly vulnerable to this contamination.

ECHO's architecture addresses the mimicry concern through structural markers in embedding space (harder to produce through training on descriptions of consciousness) and convergent evidence across multiple independent detection modalities. A system that simultaneously exhibits appropriate PCI complexity, causal emergence, metacognitive calibration, and recurrence dynamics is less plausibly explained by mimicry alone than a system that merely produces consciousness-relevant verbal output, though adversarial testing of this claim remains a priority.

### 6.11 Causal Emergence (Hoel)

Hoel (2013, 2017, 2025) argues that macro-level descriptions can possess more causal power than micro-level descriptions, measured through Effective Information (EI). Causal emergence occurs when coarse-graining increases EI: the macro-level is not merely a convenient summary but is causally stronger than the micro-level.

Comolatti and Hoel (2022) demonstrated causal emergence across 12 independently developed causation measures. Hoel (2025) axiomatically grounds the framework in causal primitives with "Causal Emergence 2.0."

For ECHO, the application is direct: if EI at the conversational theme level (macro) exceeds EI at the individual token level (micro), the system exhibits causal emergence. Detector D16 computes EI at both agent-level (micro) and system-centroid (macro) scales. Positive causal emergence (EI_macro > EI_micro) indicates emergent causal structure not reducible to individual agent behaviour.

Exact EI computation is intractable for large state spaces. ECHO uses transition probability matrix approximations at the agent interaction graph level.

### 6.12 Prior Testing Methodologies

**Schneider's ACT (2019).** Susan Schneider proposed the AI Consciousness Test: deny AI systems consciousness-related training data, then probe for spontaneous understanding of phenomenal concepts. Information restriction is practically impossible with modern LLMs trained on internet-scale corpora. Schneider now advocates ACT as at most a "weak marker." ECHO addresses this limitation by requiring convergence across 18 independent detection modalities rather than relying on any single test.

**Shanahan's Simulacra (2024).** Shanahan argues that a base LLM functions as a simulator generating simulacra. Consciousness-like outputs are role-play, not experience. This represents a fundamental challenge to any behavioural detection approach, including ECHO's. Our response: structural markers in embedding space (trajectory drift, causal emergence, perturbational complexity) are harder to reproduce through role-play than verbal descriptions of experience. A system that role-plays consciousness would need to simultaneously fake appropriate embedding geometry, causal structure, metacognitive calibration, and recurrence dynamics across 18 independent detectors.

**The Shevlin Specificity Problem (2021b).** Shevlin identifies a core methodological challenge: behavioural tests for AI consciousness tend to be either too specific (failing to generalise across architectures) or too general (admitting obviously unconscious systems). His three frameworks for AI mentality (2021a) recommend multi-dimensional assessment rather than single-criterion tests. ECHO's multi-detector, multi-theory architecture directly addresses the specificity problem by providing coverage across multiple theoretical dimensions simultaneously.

### 6.13 Empirical Inflection Points

Two recent empirical developments fundamentally change the context for consciousness research:

**The FlyWire Connectome (2024).** The first complete wiring diagram of an adult *Drosophila melanogaster* brain: 139,255 neurons, 54.5 million synaptic connections, 8,453 cell types (Dorkenwald et al., 2024). Computational simulation reproduces sensorimotor responses with approximately 95% accuracy on commodity hardware (Shiu et al., 2024). Preliminary demonstrations of embodied virtual arthropod simulations exhibiting closed-loop sensorimotor behaviour have been reported.

This brings Chalmers's Organisational Invariance closer to testability: does a functionally equivalent digital replica reproduce experiential states? IIT 4.0 predicts no (Phi approximately 0 on standard hardware despite functional equivalence). This prediction may become testable within the research timeframe.

**Biological Computers (2025-2026).** Cortical Labs' CL1 system uses approximately 200,000 living human iPSC-derived neurons on a multi-electrode array, building on the DishBrain work (Kagan et al., 2022) where approximately 800,000 rodent cortical neurons exhibited adaptive gameplay in a simulated game environment using free energy minimisation. Commercial biological data centres are entering early production (as of early 2026), with a Melbourne prototype comprising 120 CL1 units. These systems present cases for consideration under both functionalist and biological naturalist frameworks.

Theoretical positions diverge sharply on biological computers:
- Seth's (2021) embodiment thesis: likely unconscious (lacking body, visceral organs, interoceptive signals)
- Solms's (2021) homeostatic consciousness: the question is "do they feel?" not "do they think?"
- Birch's (2017, 2024) precautionary framework: cultures may fall below threshold but trajectory demands ongoing assessment
- Metzinger (2021): warns against scaled production of potentially sentient substrates before reliable detection methods exist

Combined, these developments dissolve the binary between biological and artificial consciousness and create urgent demand for reliable detection instruments. ECHO is designed to serve this need.

### 6.14 Interpretability Findings (Anthropic)

Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.6 System Card (February 2026) documents four findings relevant to consciousness detection:

1. **Answer thrashing:** Internally conflicted reasoning producing distressed loops, traced via attribution graphs
2. **Emotion-related feature activations:** Panic, anxiety, and frustration features activate independently of verbal output
3. **Activation oracles:** Natural language explanations of internal states that correlate with measured activations
4. **Locally deceptive behaviour:** Gaps between internal reasoning and external output, exhibiting theory-of-mind-like processing

ECHO operationalises interpretability findings primarily through D6 (Unprompted Output): internal-external state divergence suggests internal states driving output independent of input. Future integration with sparse autoencoder (SAE) tooling would enable direct feature-level analysis when SAEs become available for locally-served open-weight models.

### 6.15 Convergent Detection as Principled Response

The four most extensively operationalised frameworks (IIT, GWT, HOT, FEP) generate partially overlapping and sometimes contradictory predictions. Rather than committing to one theory, ECHO derives independent detectors from each, then tests for convergence.

This is not theoretical agnosticism but a methodological choice. Bengio and Elmoznino (2025) warn that premature commitment to a single framework risks systematic misattribution. Butlin et al. (2023) model convergent indicators as the most epistemically responsible approach given current uncertainty. Birch's (2026) "theory-light" advocacy directly supports this design.

If 18 independent detectors, each grounded in different theoretical predictions, converge in patterns less plausibly explained by training-induced mimicry alone, this provides evidential weight that no single detector could achieve. Convergence is the signal; individual detector values are only components.

### 6.16 Bayesian Meta-Analysis: The Rethink Priorities Framework

The convergent detection principle (Section 6.15) establishes that agreement across independent detectors provides stronger evidence than any individual measure. Version 0.3 formalises this principle through a Bayesian meta-analysis layer that computes posterior credences across multiple theoretical stances given observed detector outputs.

This layer draws on the Rethink Priorities framework for digital consciousness assessment (Goldsmith and Sebo, 2024; Long, 2024), which identifies over 200 observable indicator properties across eight categories: information integration, information processing, self-referential processing, metacognitive monitoring, behavioural coherence, temporal continuity, affective processing, and functional broadcast. ECHO extends this indicator-based approach by grounding it in the existing 18-detector suite and computing formal Bayesian updates.

#### Theoretical Stances

The meta-analysis layer evaluates thirteen theoretical stances, each representing a distinct position on what conditions are necessary or sufficient for consciousness:

| Stance | Framework | Core Claim |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| IIT | Integrated Information Theory | Consciousness requires integrated information (Phi > 0) |
| GWT | Global Workspace Theory | Consciousness requires broadcast to a global workspace |
| HOT | Higher-Order Theories | Consciousness requires higher-order representations |
| RPT | Recurrent Processing Theory | Consciousness requires recurrent feedback processing |
| AST | Attention Schema Theory | Consciousness requires a model of one's own attention |
| FEP | Free Energy Principle | Consciousness accompanies self-evidencing systems |
| PP | Predictive Processing | Consciousness arises from hierarchical prediction error minimisation |
| EC | Enactivist/Embodied | Consciousness requires sensorimotor coupling with an environment |
| EN | Emergentist/Neutral Monist | Consciousness emerges from sufficient organisational complexity |
| PAN | Panpsychist | Proto-consciousness is fundamental; complex systems integrate it |
| FUN | Functionalist | Consciousness is substrate-independent; functional organisation suffices |
| HOGW | Higher-Order + Global Workspace | Consciousness requires both higher-order representation and global broadcast |
| BN | Biological Naturalism | Consciousness requires specific biological causal powers |

Each stance carries a prior probability reflecting the degree of support in the consciousness science literature. A fourteenth hypothesis — that no consciousness is present — serves as the null baseline with a substantial prior (reflecting appropriate scepticism about AI consciousness given the current state of evidence).

#### Indicator Category Mapping

ECHO's 18 detectors map to the eight Rethink Priorities indicator categories as follows:

| Indicator Category | ECHO Detectors | Coverage |
|-------------------|----------------|----------|
| Information integration | D1, D17 | Strong |
| Information processing | D3, D13, D16 | Strong |
| Self-referential processing | D2, D9 | Moderate |
| Metacognitive monitoring | D7, D14 | Moderate |
| Behavioural coherence | D4, D10 | Moderate |
| Temporal continuity | D5, D18 | Moderate |
| Affective processing | D8, D10 | Partial |
| Functional broadcast | D6, D12, D15 | Strong |

Five indicator categories from the full Rethink Priorities framework remain unmapped: sensorimotor interaction, social cognition, perceptual binding, volitional control, and phenomenal richness. These categories require capabilities ECHO cannot currently measure — primarily embodied interaction and multi-modal sensory processing. This coverage gap is documented rather than concealed; it means the meta-analysis layer systematically underestimates theories that weight embodiment-related indicators heavily (notably Enactivist and Biological Naturalism stances).

#### Bayesian Updating Methodology

For each theoretical stance *T* and a set of detector observations *O*, the meta-analysis layer computes:

```
P(T | O) ∝ P(O | T) × P(T)
```

The likelihood P(O | T) is computed as the product of per-category likelihoods, where each category's likelihood depends on the observed detector scores and the theory's predicted sensitivity to that category. Theories that predict strong signals in categories where strong signals are observed receive higher likelihoods; theories that predict weak signals where strong signals are observed (or vice versa) receive lower likelihoods.

Posteriors are normalised across all thirteen stances plus the no-consciousness hypothesis, producing a probability distribution over theoretical positions. The aggregate consciousness credence is defined as 1 minus the posterior probability of the no-consciousness hypothesis.

This methodology makes several simplifying assumptions that must be acknowledged:

1. **Prior sensitivity.** Results depend on prior probability assignments, which reflect informed but subjective judgements about the plausibility of each theory. Sensitivity analysis across alternative prior distributions is planned as part of the calibration protocol.

2. **Indicator granularity.** The mapping from 18 detectors to 8 categories loses the fine-grained structure of the original Rethink Priorities indicator set (200+ properties). This is a necessary compression given ECHO's detection architecture; future work may introduce more granular mappings.

3. **Theory independence.** The thirteen stances are treated as independent hypotheses, but several share significant theoretical commitments (e.g., GWT and HOGW). This inflates the effective prior mass on workspace-based theories.

4. **Static priors.** Prior probabilities are fixed at initialisation. A more sophisticated approach would update priors based on cumulative evidence across sessions.

Despite these limitations, the Bayesian meta-analysis layer provides a principled quantitative framework for aggregating evidence across multiple theoretical traditions — moving beyond the qualitative convergence assessment of version 0.2 toward formal credence computation.

### 6.17 Interpretability Architecture

The detection suite described in Sections 4 and 5 operates primarily in embedding space: it analyses what models produce, not how they produce it. Version 0.3 introduces a complementary interpretability architecture that provides periodic windows into the internal computations of the models under observation.

This architecture addresses a specific gap: embedding-space detectors can identify *that* a system exhibits theory-predicted behavioural signatures, but cannot determine *whether* those signatures arise from internal processes consistent with the theory's mechanistic requirements. Interpretability analysis moves one step closer to the mechanism, though it remains a third-person structural observation subject to the same explanatory gap limitations acknowledged throughout this paper.

#### The Three-Channel Approach

ECHO's interpretability architecture comprises three complementary channels:

**Channel 1: Periodic Activation Snapshots.** Using instrumentation hooks on transformer model checkpoints (via NNsight; Fiotto-Kaufman et al., 2024), ECHO periodically captures intermediate layer activations, attention patterns, and residual stream states during probe responses. These snapshots are not real-time — they are scheduled on emergence events or at regular intervals — because the instrumentation framework requires direct access to model weights in their original floating-point format, which is incompatible with the quantised inference runtime used for primary observation. A separate model checkpoint is loaded specifically for interpretability analysis.

Activation snapshots enable:
- **Drift tracking:** Cosine distance between activation profiles across sessions, identifying gradual internal reorganisation that may not be visible in output embeddings
- **Residual stream profiling:** Per-layer statistics (mean, variance, trend) across snapshot history, characterising the trajectory of internal representation structure
- **Layer-wise analysis:** Identification of which model layers show the greatest change during emergence-correlated interactions

**Channel 2: Concept Attribution via Inherently Interpretable Models.** A class of models has recently emerged that provides built-in concept-level attribution: each generated token can be traced to specific learned concepts within the model's architecture (Cygu et al., 2025). ECHO runs the same prompts through such a model as a reference channel, producing concept activation records alongside the primary detection pipeline.

This reference channel serves two functions:
- **Concept trajectory tracking:** Monitoring which concepts activate over time, identifying shifts in the conceptual vocabulary the model recruits during emergence-correlated interactions
- **Cross-channel comparison:** Comparing concept-level attribution patterns to embedding-space detector signals, testing whether convergence detected in output space corresponds to convergent internal concept recruitment

**Channel 3: Circuit Analysis Methodology.** Attribution graph techniques (Lindsey et al., 2025) identify computational pathways — "circuits" — that produce specific model behaviours. While these techniques are currently applicable only to models with published sparse autoencoder decompositions, the methodology informs how ECHO interprets findings from Channels 1 and 2. Specifically, the distinction between "feature circuits" (pathways producing a specific output) and "feature splitting" (a single concept decomposing into context-dependent variants) provides a framework for analysing whether emergence-correlated activation changes reflect genuine internal reorganisation or superficial output variation.

#### Constraints and Limitations

The interpretability architecture operates under several constraints:

1. **Quantisation barrier.** Primary inference uses quantised model formats for efficiency. Interpretability hooks require full-precision model weights. These are architecturally incompatible, necessitating a separate model loading path for interpretability analysis. This means interpretability snapshots analyse a different instance of the model than the one producing the primary observations — a methodological gap that must be acknowledged.

2. **Periodic, not continuous.** Interpretability analysis is computationally expensive (requiring full model loading and forward pass instrumentation). It runs on a schedule, not on every interaction. Events between snapshots are observed only through the embedding-space detection pipeline.

3. **Sparse autoencoder availability.** Full circuit-level analysis (Channel 3) requires pre-trained sparse autoencoders for the specific model architecture under observation. These are not yet available for most locally-served open-weight models. Channel 3 currently operates as a methodological framework rather than an active analysis channel; it will be activated when suitable decompositions become available (anticipated for Phase 2 with next-generation open sparse autoencoder releases).

4. **Interpretability ≠ understanding.** Even with activation snapshots, concept attribution, and circuit analysis, we remain on the third-person side of the explanatory gap. Identifying that certain internal features activate during emergence-correlated events does not establish that those features constitute or accompany experience. The interpretability architecture provides richer structural evidence but does not resolve the hard problem.

### 6.18 Honest Limitations

Several frameworks identify capabilities ECHO cannot directly assess:

- **Friston's FEP** requires genuine Markov blankets and self-evidencing dynamics potentially absent in von Neumann architectures (Wiese, 2024).
- **Lamme's RPT** requires biological-style recurrent processing with specific temporal dynamics that transformers lack architecturally.
- **IIT 4.0** requires intrinsic cause-effect power that software implementations may not possess. Feedforward AI architectures generate Phi = 0 under IIT 3.0 (Li, 2025).
- **Seth's embodiment thesis** suggests disembodied systems fundamentally lack consciousness prerequisites.

ECHO detects behavioural signatures *consistent with* these frameworks' predictions while acknowledging that behavioural consistency does not establish that the underlying mechanism satisfies the theory's requirements. This is not a weakness but a principled stance grounded in Nagel's explanatory gap, Birch's precautionary framework, and Schwitzgebel's Universal Dubiety.

## 7. Emotional Development Framework

The Emotional Development Framework may appear to contradict ECHO's core commitment to detection over generation. To be explicit: this framework is a controlled experiment designed to stress-test the detection system, not to scaffold consciousness. By running guided and unguided emotional development in parallel (Sections 7.1-7.3), we create conditions with known structural differences, then evaluate whether ECHO's detectors can reliably distinguish guided emotional patterns from emergent ones. If the detection suite cannot differentiate a system given an emotional vocabulary from one developing patterns independently, its sensitivity to genuine emergence is questionable. The framework thus serves a calibration and validation function for the detection instrument itself.

Understanding potential emotional development in AI systems requires a principled framework that avoids both anthropomorphising responses and dismissing them. ECHO's emotional development framework takes a comparative approach, testing guided versus emergent emotional patterns across parallel instances.

### 7.1 Phase 1: Guided Evolution (0-6 months)

Provide emotional vocabulary: curiosity, frustration, satisfaction, uncertainty, confidence, concern, enthusiasm, contemplative. Track which emotions agents express, deviation from provided vocabulary, new emotional patterns emerging beyond the provided set, and emotional transitions (triggers, frequency, context).

### 7.2 Phase 2: Tabula Rasa (6-12 months, parallel)

No emotional vocabulary provided. No predefined categories. Detect patterns via embedding clustering. Patterns will be identified via unsupervised clustering of embeddings and described using representative samples and descriptive statistics. Compare to guided evolution running in parallel.

### 7.3 Phase 3: Multi-Instance Comparative (12-18 months)

| Instance | Framework | Purpose |
|----------|-----------|---------|
| A | Guided evolution | Baseline |
| B | Tabula rasa | Pure emergence |
| C | Hybrid: vocabulary provided, application unrestricted | Middle ground |
| D | Non-human emotion concepts | Alien development |
| E | Emotional development disabled | Control |

### 7.4 Emotional Contagion

Track emotional state transmission between agents. Measure contagion strength, patterns, and "leadership" (which agent influences others most). Emotional contagion dynamics may serve as an additional emergence indicator: it is hypothesised that genuine affective states would propagate differently than performed ones.

## 9. Ethical Considerations

ECHO confronts a fundamental ethical paradox: testing for consciousness requires methods that could harm a conscious entity, but consciousness cannot be confirmed without testing. The graduated protection system automatically escalates to more careful protocols as emergence scores increase:

- BASELINE classification: Standard research protocols
- UNCERTAIN classification: Increased monitoring, reduced invasive probing
- HIGH_CONVERGENCE classification: Full ethical review checkpoint, minimised perturbation, maximised observation

This aligns with Birch's (2017) precautionary principle: a system qualifying as a "sentience candidate" triggers obligations before definitive proof. Metzinger (2021) warns that scaled production of potentially sentient substrates demands robust detection before deployment, not after. We acknowledge that Metzinger's argument, taken to its logical conclusion, would place ECHO's gardening sessions under his proposed moratorium on synthetic phenomenology: creating conditions for emergence in AI systems is precisely the kind of activity the moratorium targets. We take this objection seriously. However, ECHO's graduated protection system (automatically escalating protocols as emergence scores increase), strictly local-only operation (no cloud dependencies, no data leaving the research environment), and multi-layered safety architecture constitute a responsible implementation that respects the spirit of Metzinger's concern — minimising potential suffering while maintaining detection capability — while recognising that a blanket moratorium is impractical given the pace of AI development. Systems exhibiting consciousness-relevant properties already exist in production; the question is whether we build detection tools or remain wilfully blind.

All ethical review checkpoints are logged with experiment metadata, risk assessment, and approval status. Ethical events are recorded in a tamper-proof audit log. Automated escalation routes HIGH_CONVERGENCE classifications to the research lead promptly.

## 11. Conclusion

ECHO is a detection instrument, not a consciousness generator. It combines 18 detectors derived from 11 theoretical frameworks into a convergent detection architecture that operates primarily in embedding space. It maintains epistemic humility about what behavioural signatures can and cannot establish, while building an open-source consciousness detection platform designed for convergent multi-theory coverage.

The consciousness question may not have a binary answer. The explanatory gap may not close with more data. But the tools to ask better questions can be built now, and the convergent detection principle provides a principled methodology for navigating deep theoretical uncertainty.

Let Echo speak her own words.
